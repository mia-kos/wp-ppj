Are you ready for the unexpected?

Every day can be different. You will need enthusiasm, determination and fast thinking to handle whatever comes your way.

Do you like helping others?

Being a prison officer is about treating others with respect, understanding and decency, and believing people can change.

Do you believe in fairness and justice?

You will need to handle situations with strength, honesty and consistency to create an environment where everyone can focus on rehabilitation.

Are you a team player?

You will need to be able to talk and listen to others, and take responsibility for the safety of those around you.

Working day

Your day can vary according to the type of prison, its level of security and the age of the prisoners. Duties can include:

  • keeping the prison secure and orderly
  • carrying out search and security checks
  • escorting and supervising prisoners
  • helping prisoners with rehabilitation programmes
  • providing support to prisoners who are vulnerable

Shift hours

You will usually work on rolling shifts of 39 hours, including some nights, weekends and public holidays (these days are added to your holiday allowance). You will usually have the opportunity for overtime, and most prisons are open to flexible working to give you a good work-life balance.

Types of prison

You may be working with women, young people, high security prisoners or mentally disordered offenders. There are no restrictions on who can work in these prisons.

The team

Your team is likely to include people with diverse experience and backgrounds. You will likely form strong bonds with your colleagues as you will be have to work together to run a safe, decent prison environment.

Holiday

You are entitled to 25 days paid holiday a year, plus 9 extra days for bank holidays. If you have to work over a public or bank holiday, these days are added to your overall holiday allowance.

Your holiday allowance rises to 30 days after 10 years of service.

Work-life balance

We offer flexible working when possible. This may depend on the working patterns of the prison and is available once you have passed your 10-week training course, which must be completed full time.

Training and probation

After induction, you will need to pass a 10-week Prison Officer Entry Level Training (POELT) course. This can either be residential or non-residential depending on your location. You will receive full pay during training.

The course helps you to prepare for life as a prison officer. When you pass you will receive an NVQ Level 3 Diploma in custodial care. This gives you a number of transferable skills including communication, looking after people in custody and legal knowledge.

Training continues throughout your 12-month probationary period. You may have to complete a further 2-week residential course if you work in a high security prison.

Career development

You will be given further training and mentoring support throughout your career, helping you to develop skills and gain experience.

You will have opportunities for professional qualifications, career progression, and to learn diverse things such as healthcare or dog handling.

As you gain experience, you could progress into management positions, specialise in different roles or move into new areas such as working with vulnerable prisoners, or with mothers and babies.

Induction

You will begin your prison officer career with an induction week where you will be assigned a mentor, meet your colleagues and learn the prison routine.
